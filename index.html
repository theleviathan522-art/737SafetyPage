The fix

What changed with MCAS — and why it matters.

The Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) kept the 737 MAX's handling consistent with older 737s. The original design had a critical flaw. Every one of those flaws has been corrected.

Original MCAS

  • ✕
    Relied on data from a single angle-of-attack sensor — one faulty reading could trigger the system
  • ✕
    Could activate repeatedly, applying more nose-down force than pilots could physically counteract
  • ✕
    MCAS was not described in pilot training materials or flight manuals
  • ✕
    No cockpit alert when the two AoA sensors disagreed with each other

Redesigned MCAS

  • ✓
    Now compares inputs from both AoA sensors — activation is blocked if sensors disagree by more than 5.5°
  • ✓
    Activates only once per event and can never apply more force than the pilot can override with the control column alone
  • ✓
    Comprehensive simulator training now required — pilots practice MCAS scenarios before ever flying the MAX
  • ✓
    AoA DISAGREE alert is always enabled and visible on primary flight displays regardless of airline option

Safety systems

Every layer of protection, explained.

The 737 MAX incorporates multiple independent safety systems — designed to catch failures before pilots are ever aware of them.

01
Dual AoA Sensor Validation
Two independent angle-of-attack sensors cross-check each other in real time. If they disagree by more than 5.5°, MCAS is immediately disabled and pilots are alerted.
02
Single-Activation Limit
MCAS can only fire once per elevated-AoA event. It can never accumulate repeated nose-down commands — a key cause of the 2018–19 accidents.
03
Pilot Override — Always
By design, MCAS will never generate more force than a pilot can overcome by pulling back on the control column alone. Pilot authority is absolute.
04
Enhanced FCC Cross-Checks
The Flight Control Computer software now performs more frequent cross-checks between its own redundant processors, catching anomalies before they can cascade.
05
AoA Disagree Alert
A permanent cockpit alert activates whenever the two AoA sensors diverge. Previously this alert was a paid airline option. It is now standard on every 737 MAX.
06
Wiring Separation
Regulators required greater physical separation of key wiring harnesses to eliminate scenarios where a single cable failure could affect multiple systems simultaneously.

Pilot readiness

What every 737 MAX pilot goes through.

Part of what made the original accidents possible was inadequate pilot knowledge of MCAS. That has fundamentally changed.

1

Computer-Based Training Modules

Pilots complete a structured suite of CBT modules covering 737 MAX flight control systems, MCAS behavior, and updated emergency procedures — before entering a simulator.

2

Full-Motion Simulator Sessions

Pilots practice handling MCAS activation scenarios in a full-motion Level D simulator — the highest-fidelity training environment available. This was not required before the grounding.

3

Knowledge Assessment

A formal proficiency check verifies each pilot's understanding of MCAS, AoA sensors, runaway stabilizer procedures, and the updated Airplane Flight Manual before they are cleared to fly.

4

737NG to MAX Differences Course

Pilots already rated on the 737 Next Generation complete a tailored differences course — ensuring no experienced pilot assumes MAX behavior is identical to the NG without understanding what changed.
